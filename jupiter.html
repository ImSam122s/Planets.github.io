Jupiter, the GIANT

Jupiter has a long history of surprising scientists – all the way back to 1610 when Galileo Galilei found the first moons beyond Earth. That discovery changed the way we see the universe. upiter's familiar stripes and swirls are actually cold, windy clouds of ammonia and water, floating in an atmosphere of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter’s iconic Great Red Spot is a giant storm bigger than Earth that has raged for hundreds of years. One spacecraft – NASA's Juno orbiter – is currently exploring this giant world.

Size and Distance

Jupiter, with a radius of 43,440.7 miles (69,911 kilometers), is approximately 11 times larger in diameter than Earth. To put it in perspective, if Earth were scaled down to the size of a nickel, Jupiter would be as big as a basketball. In terms of its distance from the Sun, Jupiter is located about 484 million miles (778 million kilometers) away on average. This puts it at a distance of 5.2 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun. An astronomical unit is the average distance between the Earth and the Sun. From this distance, it takes approximately 43 minutes for sunlight to travel from the Sun to reach Jupiter.

Moons of Jupiter
